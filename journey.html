My Journey

University of Rhode Island

Today, I'm a first-semester junior at the University of Rhode Island pursuing a BS in Computer Science. I'm living on-campus, and I've loved the friendly and inspiring vibe of the campus. I'm also offering private tutoring to students and self-learners from any background. All you need is a willingness to learn!


My tutoring is not affiliated with the University of Rhode Island in any way. I am not partnered with or affiliated with URI beyond my enrolled as a student.

Tutoring at CCRI

While I was a student at CCRI, some of my professors recommended me to the Student Success Center, which provides tutoring and academic coaching to help students achieve success. The director of the Student Success Center, Joanne Galliano, reached out to me and asked if I wanted to become a peer tutor. At first I wasn't sure, since I had never done any tutoring professionally. After some consideration, I decided to give it a shot and become a tutor. This was one of the best decisions I've ever made! I absolutely loved being able to share my knowledge to help build the confidence and understanding of my peers. I tutored math subjects up to pre-calculus, as well as many computer science focused classes. Unfortunately, only CCRI students are able to be peer tutors, so when I transferred I had to part ways with the Student Success Center. It was an incredible experience, and I'm thankful that it showed me how fulfilling and enjoyable tutoring can be.

Running Start

In my senior year of high school, I participated in a program called "Running Start", which allowed me to take college-level classes full time. These would count for both high school and college credits at the same time. This let me graduate high school with a full year's worth of college credits from the Community College of Rhode Island, where I later completed my Associate's degree before transferring to the University of Rhode Island.

Discovering Computer Science

One of the after-school activities offered at The Met was an intro HTML class. I thought it seemed interesting, so I signed up. It quickly became the class I looked forward to the most. The teacher took notice of my interest, and recommended learning Javascript as a next step. After many months of study and practice, I began to feel confident in my Javascript abilities. I was able to make small games and programs, and was still continuing to learn. Since then I've mastered Javascript, as well as learning Java, Python, C++, Node.js, Lua, and Processing.

High School

I went to a high school in Providence called The Met, which focuses on learning through real-world experiences and exploring your interests. One of the ways to get real-world experiences at The Met is through internships. Starting in my freshman year, I interned with the Center for Innovation and Entrepreneurship. With the support of my mentor Jodie Woodruff (the director of the center) I was able to learn about the business world and explore my entrepreneurial side in a supportive environment. Over the first two years of high school, I had the opportunity to share my experience at talks and conferences including the SxSW education conference in Austin Texas, The Big Bang conference in Orlando, and many more, from Boston to Ohio. I also explored computer history, hardware, and code in my internship with the Rhode Island Computer Museum.

Early Education

I went to a public elementary school, and in my time there I became familiar with what works and what doesn't in our current education system. In order to focus my studies and ensure my classes were challenging enough, my parents decided to home school me throughout middle school. That experience taught me how to learn independently, and push myself outside of my comfort zone.
